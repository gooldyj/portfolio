PROJECT OVERVIEW

The mini-course is structured to provide participants with a clear understanding of what service learning is, how it differs from community service, and how it can be integrated into academic courses to enhance student engagement and civic responsibility. Course components include:

  • Foundational Modules: An introduction to service learning theory, benefits, and alignment with institutional learning outcomes.
  • Practical Applications: Case studies drawn from DC3 service-learning initiatives such as food security projects, animal welfare partnerships, and civic advocacy campaigns.
  • Interactive Elements: Multimedia lessons, scenario-based activities, and reflective prompts designed to promote active learning and practical skill-building.
  • Assessment and Reflection: Embedded quizzes and reflection assignments that measure comprehension and encourage application of service learning concepts in real instructional contexts.

This project integrates technology-enhanced learning strategies, including multimedia creation, AI-assisted scripting, and digital delivery platforms, to model best practices in instructional design. It also reflects my commitment to inclusive education by ensuring that content is accessible, adaptable, and relevant to diverse learners.

The Service Learning Mini-Course represents a sustainable resource for DC3, equipping faculty and students with the tools to connect academic learning to community impact. It embodies my broader vision of education as a transformative process that prepares learners to think critically, act ethically, and engage meaningfully with the world around them.

METHODOLOGY

The design and development of the Service Learning Mini-Course followed an instructional design methodology grounded in the ADDIE framework:

1. Analysis

  • Identified the need for a standardized, accessible introduction to service learning at Dodge City Community College.
  • Conducted a review of institutional goals, Common Learning Outcomes, and stakeholder expectations to ensure alignment.
  • Analyzed learner characteristics, including faculty, staff, and students with varying levels of familiarity with service learning.

2. Design

  • Established learning objectives focused on defining service learning, differentiating it from community service, and demonstrating its application in academic contexts.
  • Selected digital delivery methods to maximize accessibility and scalability.
  • Designed multimedia components, quizzes, and reflection activities to promote engagement and measure outcomes.

3. Development

  • Created instructional materials including video modules (scripted with AI assistance), interactive activities, and reflective prompts.
  • Incorporated examples from DC3 service-learning initiatives (e.g., food security drives, animal welfare partnerships, microchipping ordinance advocacy).
  • Ensured accessibility through clear formatting, closed captioning, and multiple means of content representation.

4. Implementation

  • Deployed the mini-course in a digital format suitable for integration into Canvas or standalone use.
  • Facilitated initial pilot with select faculty and student participants for feedback and validation.

5. Evaluation

  • Built in formative assessments (quizzes, reflections) to measure learner comprehension.
  • Collected feedback from pilot participants to refine course clarity, relevance, and accessibility.
  • Established plans for summative evaluation based on learner outcomes and faculty adoption in future semesters.

EXPECTED OUTCOMES

The Service Learning Mini-Course is designed to achieve the following outcomes:

1. Knowledge and Awareness

  • Participants will develop a clear understanding of what service learning is and how it differs from traditional volunteerism or community service.
  • Faculty and students will recognize the academic, personal, and civic benefits of integrating service learning into coursework.

2. Application of Concepts

  • Faculty participants will be able to identify opportunities within their own courses to incorporate service learning projects aligned with course objectives and Common Learning Outcomes.
  • Students will be able to articulate how service learning connects classroom knowledge to real-world challenges.

3. Skill Development

  • Learners will practice reflective thinking, linking experiences to broader social, cultural, and ethical contexts.
  • Faculty will gain practical tools for planning, implementing, and assessing service learning initiatives.

4. Community Impact

  • The course will strengthen institutional partnerships with community organizations by preparing students and faculty to engage in meaningful, sustainable projects.
  • It will serve as a catalyst for expanding DC3's role in addressing regional issues such as food insecurity, animal welfare, and civic advocacy.

5. Sustainability

  • The mini-course will function as a reusable, scalable resource that can be integrated into orientation, professional development, or academic courses.
  • It will establish a foundation for ongoing growth of service learning at DC3, supported by consistent training and shared institutional vision.
